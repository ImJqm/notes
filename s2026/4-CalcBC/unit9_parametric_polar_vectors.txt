AP CALCULUS BC - UNIT 9: PARAMETRIC EQUATIONS, POLAR COORDINATES, AND VECTOR-VALUED FUNCTIONS (BC ONLY)
Exam Weighting: 11-12% BC
=================================================

PARAMETRIC EQUATIONS
A curve in the plane defined by x = x(t) and y = y(t) where t is the parameter.
dy/dx (slope of tangent line) = (dy/dt) / (dx/dt), provided dx/dt is not 0.
This follows from the chain rule: dy/dx = (dy/dt) * (dt/dx) = (dy/dt) / (dx/dt).

SECOND DERIVATIVE OF PARAMETRIC CURVES
d^2y/dx^2 = [d/dt(dy/dx)] / (dx/dt)
That is: differentiate dy/dx with respect to t, then divide by dx/dt.
Do NOT differentiate dy/dt and divide by d^2x/dt^2.

ARC LENGTH OF A PARAMETRIC CURVE
L = integral from t1 to t2 of sqrt[(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2] dt

VECTOR-VALUED FUNCTIONS
Position vector: r(t) = <x(t), y(t)>
Velocity vector: r'(t) = v(t) = <x'(t), y'(t)>
Acceleration vector: r''(t) = a(t) = <x''(t), y''(t)>
Speed: |v(t)| = sqrt[(x'(t))^2 + (y'(t))^2]

INTEGRATING VECTOR-VALUED FUNCTIONS
Integrate component by component:
  integral of r(t) dt = <integral of x(t) dt, integral of y(t) dt> + C vector
Given initial position r(t_0) and velocity function v(t), find position by integrating v(t) and using initial condition.

SOLVING MOTION PROBLEMS IN THE PLANE
Displacement vector from t1 to t2: integral from t1 to t2 of v(t) dt
Total distance traveled: integral from t1 to t2 of |v(t)| dt = integral of speed dt
Speed increasing: the magnitude of velocity is increasing, which happens when v dot a > 0 (same direction).
Direction of motion is given by the velocity vector.

POLAR COORDINATES
A point is described by (r, theta) where r is the distance from the origin and theta is the angle from the positive x-axis.
Conversions:
  x = r*cos(theta)
  y = r*sin(theta)
  r^2 = x^2 + y^2
  tan(theta) = y/x

DIFFERENTIATING IN POLAR FORM
For r = f(theta):
  x = f(theta) * cos(theta)
  y = f(theta) * sin(theta)
  dx/dtheta = f'(theta)*cos(theta) - f(theta)*sin(theta)
  dy/dtheta = f'(theta)*sin(theta) + f(theta)*cos(theta)
  dy/dx = (dy/dtheta) / (dx/dtheta)
The slope of the tangent line at (r, theta) = dy/dx computed as above.

AREA OF A POLAR REGION
Area bounded by r = f(theta) from theta = a to theta = b:
  A = (1/2) * integral from a to b of [f(theta)]^2 dtheta
This uses the idea that a small sector of angle d(theta) has area approximately (1/2)*r^2*d(theta).

AREA BETWEEN TWO POLAR CURVES
Area between r = f(theta) (outer) and r = g(theta) (inner) from a to b:
  A = (1/2) * integral from a to b of ([f(theta)]^2 - [g(theta)]^2) dtheta
Find the limits by solving f(theta) = g(theta) for their intersections (check also for origin crossings).

COMMON POLAR CURVES TO KNOW
Circles: r = a (circle of radius a centered at origin); r = 2a*cos(theta) (circle tangent to y-axis); r = 2a*sin(theta) (circle tangent to x-axis)
Cardioids: r = a(1 + cos theta) or r = a(1 + sin theta)
Rose curves: r = a*cos(n*theta) or r = a*sin(n*theta) (n petals if n is odd; 2n petals if n is even)
Limacons: r = a + b*cos(theta)
Lemniscates: r^2 = a^2 * cos(2*theta)

KEY EXAM TIPS
- For dy/dx in parametric: (dy/dt)/(dx/dt). For d^2y/dx^2: [d/dt(dy/dx)] / (dx/dt).
- For polar area: factor of 1/2 in front, and the integrand is r^2 (not r).
- When finding intersections of polar curves, check whether the origin (r=0) is also an intersection.
- For particle motion in the plane: distinguish between displacement vector and total distance.
- Position = initial position + integral of velocity from start time to t.
- Speed = |velocity vector|, which is always non-negative.

AP CALCULUS BC - UNIT 6: INTEGRATION AND ACCUMULATION OF CHANGE
Exam Weighting: 17-20% BC
=================================================

ACCUMULATION OF CHANGE
The area between a rate-of-change function and the x-axis over an interval gives the total accumulated change.
If the rate is positive, accumulation is positive; if negative, accumulation is negative.
Unit of area = unit of f(x) times unit of x.

RIEMANN SUMS
Approximate the area under f on [a, b] by dividing into n subintervals and summing rectangles.
Left Riemann sum: use left endpoint of each subinterval as height.
Right Riemann sum: use right endpoint of each subinterval as height.
Midpoint Riemann sum: use midpoint of each subinterval as height.
Trapezoidal sum: use average of left and right endpoint values as height (trapezoids, not rectangles).
Partitions can be uniform (equal widths) or nonuniform.

OVER/UNDERESTIMATES
If f is increasing: left Riemann sum underestimates, right Riemann sum overestimates.
If f is decreasing: left Riemann sum overestimates, right Riemann sum underestimates.
If f is concave up: trapezoidal sum overestimates; midpoint sum underestimates.
If f is concave down: trapezoidal sum underestimates; midpoint sum overestimates.

DEFINITE INTEGRAL NOTATION
The definite integral of f from a to b:
  integral from a to b of f(x) dx = lim (as max delta_x_i -> 0) of sum f(x_i*) * delta_x_i
where the sum is over all subintervals and x_i* is any point in the ith subinterval.
The definite integral of a continuous function over [a, b] equals the signed area between f and the x-axis.

PROPERTIES OF DEFINITE INTEGRALS
- integral of c*f(x) dx = c * integral of f(x) dx
- integral of [f(x) + g(x)] dx = integral of f(x) dx + integral of g(x) dx
- integral from a to a of f(x) dx = 0
- integral from a to b of f(x) dx = -integral from b to a of f(x) dx
- integral from a to b of f(x) dx + integral from b to c of f(x) dx = integral from a to c of f(x) dx
The definition of the definite integral can be extended to functions with removable or jump discontinuities.

FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF CALCULUS, PART 1 (Accumulation function)
If f is continuous on an interval containing a, then:
  d/dx [integral from a to x of f(t) dt] = f(x)
The derivative of an accumulation function is the integrand evaluated at the upper limit.
With chain rule: d/dx [integral from a to g(x) of f(t) dt] = f(g(x)) * g'(x)

FUNDAMENTAL THEOREM OF CALCULUS, PART 2 (Evaluation)
If f is continuous on [a, b] and F is an antiderivative of f:
  integral from a to b of f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)
An antiderivative of f is any function F where F'(x) = f(x).

INDEFINITE INTEGRALS (ANTIDERIVATIVES)
integral of f(x) dx = F(x) + C, where F'(x) = f(x) and C is any constant.
Always include +C for indefinite integrals.

BASIC ANTIDERIVATIVE RULES (reverse of differentiation)
integral of x^n dx = x^(n+1)/(n+1) + C for n not equal to -1
integral of 1/x dx = ln|x| + C
integral of e^x dx = e^x + C
integral of sin x dx = -cos x + C
integral of cos x dx = sin x + C
integral of sec^2 x dx = tan x + C
integral of sec x tan x dx = sec x + C
integral of csc^2 x dx = -cot x + C
integral of csc x cot x dx = -csc x + C
integral of 1/sqrt(1-x^2) dx = arcsin x + C
integral of 1/(1+x^2) dx = arctan x + C

U-SUBSTITUTION
Used when the integrand is a composite function times the derivative of the inner function.
For indefinite integrals:
  Let u = g(x), then du = g'(x) dx. Rewrite and integrate in terms of u.
For definite integrals:
  Either change the limits of integration to correspond to u, or find the antiderivative and then evaluate at original limits.

LONG DIVISION AND COMPLETING THE SQUARE
For rational integrands where degree of numerator >= degree of denominator: use polynomial long division first.
For quadratic denominators that are not factorable: complete the square, then use arctan formula.

INTEGRATION BY PARTS (BC only)
integral of u dv = u*v - integral of v du
Choose u and dv strategically. Common guideline: u should be easier to differentiate (LIATE: Logarithm, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential).
May require multiple applications or solving for the integral.

LINEAR PARTIAL FRACTIONS (BC only)
For rational functions with factorable (linear, nonrepeating) denominators:
  Decompose into sum of simpler fractions, then integrate each term.
Example: 1/[(x+1)(x-2)] = A/(x+1) + B/(x-2). Solve for A and B, then integrate.

IMPROPER INTEGRALS (BC only)
An improper integral has one or both limits infinite, or the integrand is unbounded on the interval.
Evaluate using limits of definite integrals.
  integral from a to infinity of f(x) dx = lim (as b -> inf) of integral from a to b of f(x) dx
If the limit is a finite number, the integral converges. If not, it diverges.

KEY EXAM TIPS
- When using a calculator to evaluate a definite integral in a free-response question, write the full integral expression including endpoints and dx before stating the numerical result.
- Include +C for all indefinite integrals.
- For u-substitution in definite integrals, be careful to change the limits of integration.
- Show antiderivatives clearly before evaluating FTC Part 2.
- Do not write strings of equal signs equating expressions that are not equal.

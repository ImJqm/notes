AP CALCULUS BC - UNIT 8: APPLICATIONS OF INTEGRATION
Exam Weighting: 6-9% BC
=================================================

AVERAGE VALUE OF A FUNCTION
The average value of a continuous function f on [a, b]:
  f_avg = (1/(b-a)) * integral from a to b of f(x) dx
This is different from the average rate of change, which is [f(b) - f(a)] / (b - a).

POSITION, VELOCITY, ACCELERATION WITH INTEGRALS
Velocity v(t) = s'(t), acceleration a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t).
Displacement (net change in position) over [a, b]:
  integral from a to b of v(t) dt = s(b) - s(a)
Total distance traveled over [a, b]:
  integral from a to b of |v(t)| dt
Net change of any quantity over [a, b] = integral of its rate of change over [a, b].

ACCUMULATION IN APPLIED CONTEXTS
If f'(t) is the rate of change of some quantity, then:
  integral from a to b of f'(t) dt = f(b) - f(a) (net change)
A function defined by an integral represents accumulated change from a starting point.

AREA BETWEEN CURVES (functions of x)
Area between f(x) and g(x) where f(x) >= g(x) on [a, b]:
  A = integral from a to b of [f(x) - g(x)] dx
Always integrate [top function - bottom function].
If the curves cross on [a, b], split the interval at intersection points and handle each part separately.
Or equivalently: A = integral from a to b of |f(x) - g(x)| dx.

AREA BETWEEN CURVES (functions of y)
Area between right curve x = f(y) and left curve x = g(y) on [c, d]:
  A = integral from c to d of [f(y) - g(y)] dy
Use this when curves are more naturally expressed as functions of y.

VOLUMES WITH KNOWN CROSS SECTIONS
For a solid whose cross sections perpendicular to the x-axis have area A(x):
  V = integral from a to b of A(x) dx
Common cross sections:
  Square with side s: A = s^2
  Rectangle with base b and height h: A = b*h
  Equilateral triangle with side s: A = (sqrt(3)/4) * s^2
  Isoceles right triangle with leg s: A = (1/2) * s^2
  Semicircle with diameter d: A = (pi/8) * d^2

DISC METHOD (solid of revolution, no hole)
Rotate the region under y = f(x) (above the x-axis) about the x-axis:
  V = pi * integral from a to b of [f(x)]^2 dx
About the y-axis using x = g(y):
  V = pi * integral from c to d of [g(y)]^2 dy
About any horizontal or vertical line: adjust the radius by subtracting the axis of rotation.
For rotation about y = k: radius = |f(x) - k|.

WASHER METHOD (solid of revolution, with hole)
Rotate the region between y = f(x) (outer) and y = g(x) (inner) about the x-axis:
  V = pi * integral from a to b of ([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2) dx
where f(x) >= g(x) >= 0.
About other axes: adjust radii accordingly.
  V = pi * integral of [(outer radius)^2 - (inner radius)^2] dx

ARC LENGTH (BC only)
For a smooth curve y = f(x) on [a, b]:
  L = integral from a to b of sqrt(1 + [f'(x)]^2) dx
Distance traveled vs arc length: arc length applies to the curve itself; for particle motion, total distance = integral of speed.

KEY EXAM TIPS
- Area is always non-negative: integrate [top - bottom] or use absolute value.
- For area problems with intersection points, find them first, then set up integrals accordingly.
- For volumes: draw a diagram and identify orientation (horizontal or vertical slices).
- For disc vs washer: use disc when the region touches the axis of rotation; use washer when there is a gap.
- For washer method: radius is measured perpendicular to the axis of rotation.
- Show the setup (integral expression) clearly before evaluating numerically with a calculator.
- Net change = integral of rate of change. Total distance = integral of speed (not velocity).

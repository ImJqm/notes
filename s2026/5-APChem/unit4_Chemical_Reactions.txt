UNIT 4: CHEMICAL REACTIONS
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
===========================

TOPIC 4.1 - INTRODUCTION TO REACTIONS

Physical change: change in properties but NOT composition (e.g., phase changes, dissolving).
Chemical change: substances transformed into new substances with different composition.
Evidence of chemical change: heat or light produced, gas formed, precipitate formed, color change.


TOPIC 4.2 - NET IONIC EQUATIONS

- All chemical and physical changes can be represented by balanced equations.
- Atoms are conserved: equal numbers of every element on both sides.
- Charge is also conserved in ionic equations.

Three forms of equations:
1. Molecular equation: shows complete formulas for all reactants and products.
2. Complete ionic equation: shows all soluble ionic compounds as separated ions.
3. Net ionic equation: eliminates spectator ions (ions that appear unchanged on both sides),
   showing only the species that actually change.

Steps to write a net ionic equation:
1. Write and balance the molecular equation.
2. Break all strong electrolytes (strong acids, strong bases, soluble salts) into ions.
3. Cancel spectator ions.
4. Write the net ionic equation with remaining species.


TOPIC 4.3 - REPRESENTATIONS OF REACTIONS

- Balanced chemical equations can be represented as particulate-level diagrams.
- Particulate models must be consistent with the balanced equation (correct ratios and
  types of atoms/molecules shown on each side).


TOPIC 4.4 - PHYSICAL AND CHEMICAL CHANGES

- Chemical processes: involve breaking and forming covalent or ionic bonds.
- Physical processes: involve only changes in intermolecular interactions (e.g., melting, boiling).
- Note: dissolving a salt in water can be argued either way, since ionic bonds break but
  IMFs (ion-dipole) form. The AP Exam treats this as a physical change unless otherwise stated.


TOPIC 4.5 - STOICHIOMETRY

- Balanced equation coefficients represent mole ratios of reactants and products.
- Stoichiometry steps:
  1. Balance the equation.
  2. Convert given quantity to moles.
  3. Use mole ratio from balanced equation.
  4. Convert moles of desired substance to requested units.

Limiting reactant:
- The reactant that is completely consumed first; it determines maximum product formed.
- Compare moles of each reactant to the mole ratio needed; the one present in lesser
  relative amount is limiting.

Percent yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) x 100

Stoichiometry can be combined with:
- Ideal gas law: PV = nRT to find moles of gas.
- Molarity: M = n/L to find moles of solute in solution.


TOPIC 4.6 - INTRODUCTION TO TITRATION

- Titration: determines amount of analyte by reacting it with a titrant of known concentration.
- Equivalence point: point at which analyte is completely consumed by the titrant.
  moles of acid = moles of base at equivalence point (for 1:1 reactions).
- Endpoint: the experimentally observed event (usually a color change of an indicator)
  signaling the titration is complete; ideally coincides with the equivalence point.
- MA x VA = MB x VB (for 1:1 acid-base reactions at equivalence point)


TOPIC 4.7 - TYPES OF CHEMICAL REACTIONS

Acid-Base reactions:
- Involve transfer of one or more protons (H+) between species.

Oxidation-Reduction (Redox) reactions:
- Involve transfer of one or more electrons between species.
- Indicated by changes in oxidation numbers.
- Oxidation: loss of electrons (oxidation number increases).
- Reduction: gain of electrons (oxidation number decreases).
- Combustion: a redox reaction where a substance reacts with O2.
  Complete combustion of hydrocarbons: produces CO2 and H2O.
- NOTE: The terms "reducing agent" and "oxidizing agent" will NOT be tested on the AP Exam.

Precipitation reactions:
- Mixing aqueous ions to form an insoluble (or sparingly soluble) ionic compound.
- Must-know solubility: all Na+, K+, NH4+, and NO3- salts are soluble in water.
- NOTE: Rote memorization of other solubility rules is NOT required.

Oxidation number rules:
1. Element in pure form = 0.
2. Monatomic ion = charge of ion.
3. O in compounds = -2 (except in peroxides = -1, or with F).
4. H in compounds = +1 (except with metals = -1).
5. Sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0.
6. Sum of oxidation numbers in a polyatomic ion = ion charge.


TOPIC 4.8 - INTRODUCTION TO ACID-BASE REACTIONS

Bronsted-Lowry definitions:
- Acid: proton (H+) donor.
- Base: proton (H+) acceptor.
- When an acid donates H+, it becomes its conjugate base.
- When a base accepts H+, it becomes its conjugate acid.
- Conjugate acid-base pairs differ by exactly one H+.

Water as amphoteric:
- Water can donate or accept protons, acting as either acid or base.
- In water: H2O + H2O <-> H3O+ + OH-

NOTE: Lewis acid-base concepts will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.


TOPIC 4.9 - OXIDATION-REDUCTION (REDOX) REACTIONS

Half-reactions:
- Oxidation half-reaction: shows the species being oxidized (electrons on right).
- Reduction half-reaction: shows the species being reduced (electrons on left).

Balancing redox equations using half-reactions (acidic solution):
1. Write unbalanced half-reactions.
2. Balance all atoms except O and H.
3. Balance O by adding H2O.
4. Balance H by adding H+.
5. Balance charge by adding electrons.
6. Multiply half-reactions so electrons cancel.
7. Add half-reactions and simplify.

For basic solution: after balancing in acid, add OH- to both sides to neutralize H+.


KEY EQUATIONS FOR UNIT 4
- MA x VA = MB x VB (acid-base titration, 1:1)
- Percent yield = (actual / theoretical) x 100

UNIT 2: COMPOUND STRUCTURE AND PROPERTIES
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
===========================================

TOPIC 2.1 - TYPES OF CHEMICAL BONDS

- Electronegativity increases left to right across a period; decreases down a group.
- Nonpolar covalent bond: electrons shared between atoms of similar electronegativity
  (e.g., C-H bonds are considered effectively nonpolar).
- Polar covalent bond: electrons shared between atoms of different electronegativity.
  The more electronegative atom carries a partial negative charge (delta-).
  Greater electronegativity difference = greater bond dipole.
  All polar bonds have some ionic character; the distinction between ionic and covalent is
  a continuum, not a sharp boundary.
- Ionic bond: typically between a metal and a nonmetal; one atom effectively transfers
  electrons to the other.
- Metallic bonding: valence electrons are delocalized across all metal atoms (sea of electrons).
  This accounts for electrical conductivity, malleability, and ductility of metals.


TOPIC 2.2 - INTRAMOLECULAR FORCE AND POTENTIAL ENERGY

- A potential energy vs. internuclear distance graph shows:
  - Equilibrium bond length: the internuclear distance at minimum potential energy.
  - Bond energy: the energy required to separate the atoms from equilibrium to infinity.
- Bond order affects bond length and energy:
  - Triple bonds are shorter and stronger than double bonds.
  - Double bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds.
- Ionic bond strength (Coulomb's law):
  - Larger ionic charges -> stronger attraction.
  - Smaller ionic radii -> stronger attraction (ions closer together).


TOPIC 2.3 - STRUCTURE OF IONIC SOLIDS

- Ionic crystals are arranged in a systematic, periodic 3D array that maximizes
  cation-anion attraction while minimizing same-charge repulsion.
- NOTE: Knowledge of specific crystal structures (NaCl, CsCl, ZnS) will NOT
  be assessed on the AP Exam.


TOPIC 2.4 - STRUCTURE OF METALS AND ALLOYS

- Metallic solid model: array of positive metal ion cores surrounded by delocalized
  (mobile) valence electrons.
- Interstitial alloy: smaller atoms fill spaces (interstices) between larger metal atoms.
  Example: steel (carbon occupies gaps in iron lattice). Tends to be harder, less malleable.
- Substitutional alloy: atoms of comparable size replace each other in the lattice.
  Example: brass (zinc replaces copper). Typically retains metallic conductivity.


TOPIC 2.5 - LEWIS DIAGRAMS

Rules for drawing Lewis structures:
1. Count total valence electrons for all atoms.
2. Arrange atoms (least electronegative atom is usually central, H always terminal).
3. Connect atoms with single bonds (each uses 2 electrons).
4. Complete octets of terminal atoms first with lone pairs.
5. Place remaining electrons on central atom.
6. If central atom lacks an octet, form double or triple bonds with adjacent atoms.
7. Exceptions to octet rule:
   - Atoms with fewer than 8 electrons: H (2), Be (4), B (6).
   - Atoms with expanded octets (beyond period 2): P, S, Cl, etc. can hold more than 8.
   - Odd-electron species (radicals) cannot have full octets everywhere.


TOPIC 2.6 - RESONANCE AND FORMAL CHARGE

- Resonance: when more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn for a molecule,
  the true structure is a hybrid of all resonance contributors. Resonance structures
  differ only in the placement of pi electrons, not sigma bonds or atoms.
- Formal charge = (valence electrons) - (nonbonding electrons) - (1/2 bonding electrons)
- The best Lewis structure minimizes formal charges and places negative formal charges
  on more electronegative atoms.
- Limitations: Lewis structures cannot accurately represent molecules with odd numbers
  of valence electrons.


TOPIC 2.7 - VSEPR AND HYBRIDIZATION

VSEPR theory: electron pairs (bonding and lone pairs) arrange themselves to minimize
repulsion. Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs.

Electron Geometry and Molecular Geometry:

2 electron groups: linear, 180 degrees bond angle, sp hybridized
3 electron groups:
  - 0 lone pairs: trigonal planar, 120 degrees, sp2
  - 1 lone pair: bent, <120 degrees, sp2
4 electron groups:
  - 0 lone pairs: tetrahedral, 109.5 degrees, sp3
  - 1 lone pair: trigonal pyramidal, <109.5 degrees, sp3
  - 2 lone pairs: bent, <109.5 degrees, sp3
5 electron groups (expanded octet):
  - 0 lone pairs: trigonal bipyramidal
  - 1 lone pair: seesaw
  - 2 lone pairs: T-shaped
6 electron groups (expanded octet):
  - 0 lone pairs: octahedral
  - 1 lone pair: square pyramidal
  - 2 lone pairs: square planar

Hybridization:
- sp: 2 electron groups, 180 degrees bond angles
- sp2: 3 electron groups, 120 degrees bond angles
- sp3: 4 electron groups, 109.5 degrees bond angles
- NOTE: Hybridization involving d orbitals will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.
  For molecules with 5 or 6 electron groups, students are only responsible for molecular shape.

Sigma and Pi bonds:
- A sigma (sigma) bond: formed by direct (end-to-end) overlap of orbitals. All single bonds
  are sigma bonds.
- A pi (pi) bond: formed by lateral (side-by-side) overlap of orbitals. All double bonds
  contain one sigma and one pi. Triple bonds contain one sigma and two pi bonds.
- Pi bonds are weaker than sigma bonds.
- Pi bonds prevent free rotation around the bond axis -> geometric (cis/trans) isomers.
- NOTE: Molecular orbital diagrams and bonding/antibonding/nonbonding orbital
  distinctions will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.

Dipole moment (polarity of molecule):
- A molecule is polar if it has polar bonds AND the bond dipoles do not cancel due
  to asymmetric geometry.
- Symmetric molecules (e.g., BF3, CCl4, CO2) are nonpolar even with polar bonds.
- Asymmetric molecules (e.g., H2O, NH3, HCl) are polar.

UNIT 3: PROPERTIES OF SUBSTANCES AND MIXTURES
AP Exam Weighting: 18-22% (largest unit on exam)
================================================

TOPIC 3.1 - INTERMOLECULAR AND INTERPARTICLE FORCES

Types (from weakest to strongest in general):

London Dispersion Forces (LDF):
- Present in ALL molecules and atoms.
- Caused by temporary, fluctuating dipoles inducing dipoles in neighboring molecules.
- Strength increases with: larger molecular size (more electrons), greater surface area.
- Often the dominant IMF in large nonpolar molecules.
- NOTE: Do NOT use "London dispersion forces" and "van der Waals forces" interchangeably.

Dipole-Dipole Interactions:
- Present between polar molecules.
- Stronger than LDF for molecules of comparable size because they add on top of LDF.
- Strength depends on the magnitudes of the dipoles and their orientation.

Ion-Dipole Forces:
- Between ions and polar molecules.
- Stronger than dipole-dipole forces.
- Important in dissolving ionic compounds in polar solvents (e.g., NaCl in water).

Hydrogen Bonding:
- A special, strong type of dipole-dipole interaction.
- Occurs when H is covalently bonded to N, O, or F and is attracted to a lone pair
  on another N, O, or F atom (in a different molecule or another region of the same molecule).
- Explains anomalously high boiling points of H2O, HF, NH3.
- Important in DNA base pairing and protein folding.

Noncovalent interactions in biomolecules:
- Large biomolecules use combinations of IMFs to maintain 3D shape and function.


TOPIC 3.2 - PROPERTIES OF SOLIDS

Ionic solids:
- Held by strong electrostatic interactions between cations and anions.
- High melting points, low vapor pressure, brittle (shatters when layers shift because
  like charges align and repel), conducts electricity only when melted or dissolved.

Covalent network solids:
- Atoms covalently bonded in continuous 3D (diamond) or 2D (graphite) networks.
- Formed from nonmetals/metalloids only.
- Very high melting points due to strong covalent bonds.
- Diamond: hard and rigid (fixed bond angles in 3D network), does not conduct electricity.
- Graphite: soft (layers slide easily), conducts electricity within layers due to delocalized pi electrons.

Molecular solids:
- Discrete covalently-bonded molecules held together by IMFs.
- Low melting points (IMFs are weak compared to covalent/ionic bonds).
- Do not conduct electricity (valence electrons are localized in bonds/lone pairs).
- Can be composed of very large molecules or polymers.

Metallic solids:
- Sea of delocalized electrons surrounding positive metal ion cores.
- Good conductors of electricity and heat (mobile electrons).
- Malleable and ductile (metal cores can slide without breaking bonds).
- Interstitial alloys: more rigid, less malleable than pure metals.
- Alloys retain conducting sea of electrons.

Biomolecules/polymers:
- Shape and function strongly depend on noncovalent interactions within and between molecules.


TOPIC 3.3 - SOLIDS, LIQUIDS, AND GASES

Solid:
- Particles in fixed positions with limited motion (vibration only).
- Crystalline: regular, repeating 3D arrangement.
- Amorphous: no regular arrangement (e.g., glass).

Liquid:
- Particles in close contact, continuously moving and colliding.
- IMF strength and temperature determine arrangement and motion.
- Definite volume, no definite shape.

Gas:
- Particles in constant random motion, widely spaced.
- No definite shape or volume.
- Collisions between particles and container walls create pressure.

NOTE: Solid and liquid have similar molar volumes because particles are always
in close contact. Gases have much larger molar volumes.

NOTE: Phase diagrams will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.


TOPIC 3.4 - IDEAL GAS LAW

EQN: PV = nRT
  P = pressure (atm or kPa)
  V = volume (L)
  n = moles of gas
  R = gas constant (0.08206 L*atm/mol*K  or  8.314 J/mol*K)
  T = temperature in Kelvin

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures:
- In a gas mixture, each gas exerts pressure independently.
- EQN: PA = Ptotal x XA  (where XA = moles A / total moles)
- EQN: Ptotal = PA + PB + PC + ...

Key relationships from PV = nRT (at constant amounts):
- Boyle's Law: P and V are inversely proportional at constant T and n.
- Charles's Law: V and T are directly proportional at constant P and n.
- Gay-Lussac's Law: P and T are directly proportional at constant V and n.
- Avogadro's Law: V and n are directly proportional at constant P and T.


TOPIC 3.5 - KINETIC MOLECULAR THEORY (KMT)

Assumptions of KMT (ideal gas):
1. Gas particles are in constant, random motion.
2. Particle volume is negligible compared to container volume.
3. No intermolecular forces between gas particles.
4. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no net energy loss).

EQN: KE = (1/2)mv^2  (kinetic energy of a single particle)

- Average KE is proportional to Kelvin temperature.
- Higher temperature = higher average speed = broader Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.
- Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: shows range of particle speeds/energies at a given T.
  At higher T: distribution shifts right (higher average speed) and flattens (wider range).
- Heavier molecules move more slowly at the same temperature than lighter molecules.


TOPIC 3.6 - DEVIATIONS FROM IDEAL GAS LAW

- Real gases deviate from ideal behavior because:
  1. Real gas particles do have intermolecular attractions (especially at low T and high P).
  2. Real gas particles do have finite volume (significant at very high P).

- At high pressure: actual volume is greater than predicted (particles take up space).
- At low temperature (near condensation): actual pressure is less than predicted
  (IMFs pull particles together, reducing collision force against walls).
- Gases deviate most from ideal behavior at LOW TEMPERATURE and HIGH PRESSURE.


TOPIC 3.7 - SOLUTIONS AND MIXTURES

- Homogeneous mixture (solution): uniform composition throughout (e.g., saltwater).
- Heterogeneous mixture: composition varies by location (e.g., sand and water).

Molarity:
- EQN: M = n(solute) / V(solution in liters)
- Units: mol/L (molar)

Dilution: M1V1 = M2V2


TOPIC 3.8 - REPRESENTATIONS OF SOLUTIONS

- Particulate representations of solutions show:
  - Relative concentrations of components (number of dissolved particles).
  - Interactions between solute and solvent particles (e.g., ion-dipole in NaCl/H2O).
- When ionic compounds dissolve: ions shown surrounded by water molecules with
  partial charges oriented appropriately (negative end of water near cation).

NOTE: Colligative properties will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.
NOTE: Calculations of molality, percent by mass, percent by volume will NOT be tested.


TOPIC 3.9 - SEPARATION OF SOLUTIONS AND MIXTURES

- Solutions cannot be separated by filtration.
- Chromatography (paper, thin-layer, column):
  - Separates compounds based on differential intermolecular interactions with
    mobile phase (solution moving through) and stationary phase (paper or solid).
  - More polar compounds: stronger interaction with polar stationary phase -> travel less.
  - Less polar compounds: travel further with nonpolar mobile phase.
  - Chromatogram can be used to infer relative polarities of components.
- Distillation: separates liquids based on differences in boiling points.


TOPIC 3.10 - SOLUBILITY

"Like dissolves like":
- Polar/ionic solutes dissolve in polar solvents (e.g., NaCl in water).
- Nonpolar solutes dissolve in nonpolar solvents (e.g., I2 in hexane).
- Substances with similar IMFs tend to be miscible (liquids) or soluble.

Ionic compounds dissolving:
- Ion-dipole interactions between ions and water molecules provide energy to
  overcome the lattice energy and dissolve the compound.


TOPIC 3.11 - SPECTROSCOPY AND THE ELECTROMAGNETIC SPECTRUM

Electromagnetic spectrum (from low to high energy):
Radio -> Microwave -> Infrared -> Visible -> Ultraviolet -> X-ray -> Gamma ray

- Microwave radiation: associated with transitions in molecular rotational energy levels.
- Infrared (IR) radiation: associated with transitions in molecular vibrational energy levels.
  Molecules must have a change in dipole moment to absorb IR.
- Ultraviolet/Visible (UV-Vis) radiation: associated with transitions in electronic energy levels
  (electrons moving between orbitals).

Absorption vs. emission:
- Absorption: photon absorbed, energy increases.
- Emission: photon emitted, energy decreases.


TOPIC 3.12 - PROPERTIES OF PHOTONS

Speed of light equation:
- EQN: c = lambda x nu
  c = speed of light (3.00 x 10^8 m/s)
  lambda = wavelength (m)
  nu = frequency (Hz or s^-1)

Planck's equation:
- EQN: E = h x nu
  E = energy of photon (J)
  h = Planck's constant (6.626 x 10^-34 J*s)
  nu = frequency (Hz)

- Therefore: shorter wavelength = higher frequency = higher energy per photon.
- When a photon is absorbed, the atom/molecule energy increases by exactly E = h*nu.
- When a photon is emitted, the atom/molecule energy decreases by exactly E = h*nu.


TOPIC 3.13 - BEER-LAMBERT LAW

EQN: A = epsilon x b x c
  A = absorbance (unitless)
  epsilon = molar absorptivity (how strongly the substance absorbs at a given wavelength)
  b = path length of light through the solution (cm)
  c = concentration of absorbing species (mol/L)

- Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration when path length and wavelength
  are held constant.
- The spectrophotometer is set to the wavelength of maximum absorbance (optimum
  wavelength) to maximize sensitivity.
- A calibration curve (absorbance vs. known concentrations) is used to determine
  unknown concentrations.
- Percent transmittance = (I/I0) x 100. Relationship: A = -log(T/100)

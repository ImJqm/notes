UNIT 7: EQUILIBRIUM
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
========================

TOPIC 7.1 - INTRODUCTION TO EQUILIBRIUM

- Many chemical processes are reversible: they can proceed in both forward and
  reverse directions simultaneously.
- Examples: dissolution/precipitation, vaporization/condensation, acid-base reactions,
  redox reactions.
- At equilibrium: no observable changes; concentrations and partial pressures of all
  species remain constant.
- Equilibrium is DYNAMIC: forward and reverse reactions continue at equal rates.
  There is no net change, but both directions are still occurring.


TOPIC 7.2 - DIRECTION OF REVERSIBLE REACTIONS

- If rate(forward) > rate(reverse): net conversion of reactants to products (proceeds forward).
- If rate(reverse) > rate(forward): net conversion of products to reactants (proceeds reverse).
- Equilibrium: rate(forward) = rate(reverse); no net change.


TOPIC 7.3 - REACTION QUOTIENT AND EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT

For the reaction: aA + bB <-> cC + dD

EQN: Kc = [C]^c [D]^d / ([A]^a [B]^b)

EQN: Kp = (PC)^c (PD)^d / ((PA)^a (PB)^b)

Kc uses molar concentrations; Kp uses partial pressures.
- Pure solids and pure liquids are NOT included in K or Q expressions (their
  concentrations/pressures are constant and incorporated into K).
- Q (reaction quotient): same expression as K, but using non-equilibrium values.

NOTE: Conversion between Kc and Kp will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.
NOTE: Equilibrium calculations for dissolved species in equilibrium with gas phase
will NOT be assessed.


TOPIC 7.4 - CALCULATING THE EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT

- Measure concentrations or partial pressures of all species at equilibrium.
- Substitute into the equilibrium expression to find K.
- ICE table method (Initial, Change, Equilibrium):
  1. Write initial concentrations.
  2. Define x as the change (increase or decrease) in concentration.
  3. Express equilibrium concentrations in terms of x.
  4. Substitute into K expression and solve for x.


TOPIC 7.5 - MAGNITUDE OF THE EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT

- Large K (K >> 1): reaction proceeds nearly to completion; products dominate at equilibrium.
- Small K (K << 1): reaction barely proceeds; reactants dominate at equilibrium.
- K = 1: roughly equal concentrations of reactants and products at equilibrium.


TOPIC 7.6 - PROPERTIES OF THE EQUILIBRIUM CONSTANT

If a reaction is reversed: K becomes 1/K (take the reciprocal).
If stoichiometric coefficients are multiplied by c: K becomes K^c.
If reactions are added: K(overall) = K1 x K2 x K3 x ...
These rules also apply to Q.


TOPIC 7.7 - CALCULATING EQUILIBRIUM CONCENTRATIONS

Comparing Q to K:
- Q < K: reaction proceeds forward (toward more products) to reach equilibrium.
- Q > K: reaction proceeds reverse (toward more reactants) to reach equilibrium.
- Q = K: system is at equilibrium; no net change occurs.

ICE table method:
1. Set up ICE table with initial concentrations.
2. Let x = change in concentration of reactants (decrease) and products (increase).
3. Write equilibrium expressions in terms of x.
4. Substitute into K expression.
5. Solve (may require quadratic formula or simplifying approximations when K is small).


TOPIC 7.8 - REPRESENTATIONS OF EQUILIBRIUM

- Particulate diagrams at equilibrium show the relative numbers of reactant and
  product molecules consistent with the value of K.
- Can also show the approach to equilibrium over time using graphs of concentration vs. time.


TOPIC 7.9 - LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE

Le Chatelier's Principle: When a system at equilibrium is disturbed (stressed), the
system shifts to counteract the disturbance and reach a new equilibrium.

Stresses and responses:

Adding reactant -> reaction shifts forward (toward products).
Removing reactant -> reaction shifts reverse (toward reactants).
Adding product -> reaction shifts reverse (toward reactants).
Removing product -> reaction shifts forward (toward products).

Changing volume/pressure (gas phase reactions only):
- Decrease volume (increase pressure) -> reaction shifts toward side with fewer moles of gas.
- Increase volume (decrease pressure) -> reaction shifts toward side with more moles of gas.
- If equal moles of gas on both sides: no shift.

Changing temperature:
- Increasing T -> reaction shifts in endothermic direction (K changes).
- Decreasing T -> reaction shifts in exothermic direction (K changes).
- Temperature changes alter the VALUE of K; all other stresses do not change K.

Adding an inert gas (no volume change): no effect on equilibrium.
Catalyst: no effect on equilibrium position or K; only speeds up reaching equilibrium.


TOPIC 7.10 - REACTION QUOTIENT AND LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE

- Any stress that shifts equilibrium changes Q relative to K, which drives the reaction
  to restore Q = K.
- Concentration change: Q changes, K stays same; system shifts to reestablish Q = K.
- Temperature change: K changes; Q initially unchanged; system shifts to achieve Q = K.


TOPIC 7.11 - INTRODUCTION TO SOLUBILITY EQUILIBRIA

For the dissolution: MA(s) <-> M+(aq) + A-(aq)
EQN: Ksp = [M+][A-]

- Ksp = solubility product constant; measures extent of dissolution.
- Larger Ksp = more soluble salt.
- Smaller Ksp = less soluble salt.

Calculating molar solubility from Ksp:
- Let s = moles per liter that dissolve.
- Express ion concentrations in terms of s using stoichiometry.
- Substitute into Ksp expression and solve for s.

Predicting precipitation:
- Calculate Q using actual ion concentrations.
- If Q > Ksp: solution is supersaturated; precipitation occurs.
- If Q < Ksp: solution is unsaturated; no precipitation.
- If Q = Ksp: solution is at saturation; equilibrium.


TOPIC 7.12 - COMMON-ION EFFECT

- The solubility of a salt DECREASES when one of its constituent ions is already present
  in solution (common-ion effect).
- Explained by Le Chatelier's principle: adding a product ion shifts equilibrium toward
  the solid (less dissolution).
- Calculation: use ICE table where initial concentration of the common ion is given,
  then substitute into Ksp to find new molar solubility.


KEY EQUATIONS FOR UNIT 7
- Kc = [C]^c[D]^d / ([A]^a[B]^b)
- Kp = (PC)^c(PD)^d / ((PA)^a(PB)^b)
- Ksp = [M+]^m[A-]^a (for dissolution equilibria)
- If reaction reversed: K(new) = 1/K
- If coefficients multiplied by c: K(new) = K^c
- If reactions added: K(overall) = K1 x K2 x ...

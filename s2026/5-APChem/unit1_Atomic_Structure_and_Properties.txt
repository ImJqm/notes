UNIT 1: ATOMIC STRUCTURE AND PROPERTIES
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
========================================

TOPIC 1.1 - MOLES AND MOLAR MASS

- Atoms and molecules cannot be counted directly in lab work.
- Avogadro's number: NA = 6.022 x 10^23 mol^-1. It connects moles to number of particles.
- The molar mass of a substance (in g/mol) is numerically equal to the average atomic or
  molecular mass in amu.
- Key equation: n = m/M  (moles = mass / molar mass)
- Use dimensional analysis to convert between mass, moles, and number of particles.


TOPIC 1.2 - MASS SPECTRA OF ELEMENTS

- A mass spectrum of a pure element shows the isotopes present and their relative abundance.
- The average atomic mass is calculated as the weighted average of isotopic masses:
  average atomic mass = sum of (isotope mass x fractional abundance) for all isotopes
- NOTE: The AP Exam only tests singly charged monatomic ions from a single element.
  Peaks from multiple elements or other species are NOT tested.


TOPIC 1.3 - ELEMENTAL COMPOSITION OF PURE SUBSTANCES

- Pure substances may be made of individual molecules, or atoms/ions in fixed ratios (formula units).
- Law of definite proportions: any pure sample of a compound always has the same ratio of
  masses of its constituent elements.
- The empirical formula shows the lowest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.
- To find empirical formula from percent composition: convert % to grams, grams to moles,
  divide all by the smallest number of moles, round to whole numbers.


TOPIC 1.4 - COMPOSITION OF MIXTURES

- Pure substances: contain only one type of atom, molecule, or formula unit.
- Mixtures: contain two or more types of particles in variable proportions.
- Elemental analysis can determine the relative numbers of atoms in a substance and
  assess purity.
- Mass percent = (mass of component / total mass) x 100


TOPIC 1.5 - ATOMIC STRUCTURE AND ELECTRON CONFIGURATION

- The atom has a positively charged nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by negatively
  charged electrons.
- Coulomb's law: the force between charged particles is proportional to the product of their
  charges divided by the square of the distance between them.
  EQN: F(coulombic) proportional to (q1 x q2) / r^2
- Electrons occupy shells (energy levels) and subshells (sublevels): s, p, d, f.
- Inner electrons = core electrons; outer electrons = valence electrons.
- Electron configurations follow the Aufbau principle: fill lowest energy subshells first.
  Order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p...
- Hund's rule: within a subshell, electrons occupy orbitals singly before pairing.
- Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons can have identical quantum numbers
  (each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins).
- For ions: remove electrons from the highest-energy subshell first (typically outermost shell).
- NOTE: Writing electron configurations for exceptions to Aufbau (e.g., Cr, Cu) will NOT
  be tested on the AP Exam.
- Ionization energy (the energy to remove an electron) increases as effective nuclear charge
  increases and as distance from nucleus decreases. Coulomb's law explains this qualitatively.


TOPIC 1.6 - PHOTOELECTRON SPECTROSCOPY (PES)

- PES measures the energy required to remove electrons from different subshells.
- Each peak in a PES spectrum corresponds to a subshell.
- Peak position (binding energy): higher binding energy means the electrons are closer to the
  nucleus or experience a greater effective nuclear charge.
- Peak height: proportional to the number of electrons in that subshell.
- PES data can be used to verify or predict electron configurations.
- Lower binding energy peaks = valence electrons (easier to remove).
- Higher binding energy peaks = core electrons (harder to remove).


TOPIC 1.7 - PERIODIC TRENDS

- The periodic table is organized by increasing atomic number; recurring properties reflect
  patterns in ground-state electron configurations.
- All trends below can be explained using Coulomb's law, the shell model, shielding, and
  effective nuclear charge (Zeff).

Ionization Energy (IE):
- Energy needed to remove the outermost electron.
- Increases across a period (left to right): more protons, same shielding, higher Zeff.
- Decreases down a group: electrons are farther from the nucleus, more shielding.
- Successive IEs increase dramatically when a core electron is removed.

Atomic and Ionic Radius:
- Atomic radius decreases across a period (higher Zeff pulls electrons closer).
- Atomic radius increases down a group (more electron shells).
- Cations are smaller than their parent atoms (fewer electrons, same/more nuclear charge).
- Anions are larger than their parent atoms (more electrons, same nuclear charge).

Electron Affinity:
- Energy change when a neutral atom gains an electron.
- Generally becomes more negative (more exothermic) across a period.
- Trends are less regular than IE.

Electronegativity:
- Tendency of an atom to attract electrons in a bond.
- Increases across a period, decreases down a group.
- Fluorine has the highest electronegativity.

- Periodicity allows prediction of unknown or unmeasured property values.


TOPIC 1.8 - VALENCE ELECTRONS AND IONIC COMPOUNDS

- Bond formation is driven by interactions between valence electrons and nuclei.
- Elements in the same group (column) have the same number of valence electrons and form
  analogous compounds (e.g., NaCl and KCl are both 1:1 ionic compounds).
- Typical ionic charges are predicted from the periodic table:
  - Group 1: +1
  - Group 2: +2
  - Group 13: +3
  - Group 15: -3
  - Group 16: -2
  - Group 17: -1
  - Transition metals: variable charges


KEY EQUATIONS FOR UNIT 1
- n = m/M  (moles = mass in grams / molar mass in g/mol)
- F(coulombic) proportional to (q1 x q2) / r^2
- Average atomic mass = sum of (fraction x isotopic mass) for all isotopes

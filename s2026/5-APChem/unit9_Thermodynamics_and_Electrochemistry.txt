UNIT 9: THERMODYNAMICS AND ELECTROCHEMISTRY
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
=============================================

TOPIC 9.1 - INTRODUCTION TO ENTROPY

- Entropy (S): a measure of the dispersal of matter and energy in a system.
- Entropy increases when matter becomes more dispersed (more disordered).

Processes that INCREASE entropy (deltaS > 0):
- Solid -> liquid -> gas (particles become freer to move)
- Dissolving a solid into solution
- Increasing temperature (broader distribution of kinetic energies)
- Increasing volume of a gas at constant temperature
- Reactions that produce more moles of gas than they consume

Processes that DECREASE entropy (deltaS < 0):
- Gas -> liquid -> solid
- Precipitation from solution
- Reactions that consume more moles of gas than they produce

General rule for reactions:
- If moles of gas(products) > moles of gas(reactants): deltaS > 0
- If moles of gas(products) < moles of gas(reactants): deltaS < 0


TOPIC 9.2 - ABSOLUTE ENTROPY AND ENTROPY CHANGE

- Absolute entropy (S degrees): standard molar entropy in J/mol*K.
  Unlike enthalpy, absolute entropies can be assigned (they are never negative for a
  real substance; S degrees = 0 only at absolute zero).
- More complex molecules have higher absolute entropy.
- Gases have higher entropy than liquids; liquids higher than solids.

EQN: deltaS degrees(reaction) = sum[S degrees(products)] - sum[S degrees(reactants)]

(multiply each S degrees by the stoichiometric coefficient)


TOPIC 9.3 - GIBBS FREE ENERGY AND THERMODYNAMIC FAVORABILITY

EQN: deltaG degrees = deltaH degrees - T*deltaS degrees
  T = temperature in Kelvin

- deltaG degrees < 0: process is thermodynamically favored (products favored at equilibrium).
- deltaG degrees > 0: process is thermodynamically unfavored (reactants favored at equilibrium).
- deltaG degrees = 0: system is at equilibrium under standard conditions.

NOTE: "Thermodynamically favored" is preferred over "spontaneous" in AP Chemistry.
NOTE: Thermodynamically favored does NOT mean fast (kinetics and thermodynamics are separate).

Predicting thermodynamic favorability from signs of deltaH and deltaS:

deltaH < 0 and deltaS > 0: deltaG < 0 at ALL temperatures (always favored)
deltaH > 0 and deltaS < 0: deltaG > 0 at ALL temperatures (never favored)
deltaH < 0 and deltaS < 0: favored at LOW temperatures (enthalpy drives favorability)
deltaH > 0 and deltaS > 0: favored at HIGH temperatures (entropy drives favorability)

At the crossover temperature: deltaG = 0, so T = deltaH degrees / deltaS degrees

Standard Gibbs free energy from formation values:
EQN: deltaG degrees(reaction) = sum[deltaGf degrees(products)] - sum[deltaGf degrees(reactants)]


TOPIC 9.4 - THERMODYNAMIC AND KINETIC CONTROL

- A process can be thermodynamically favored (deltaG < 0) but still not occur at a
  measurable rate.
- Kinetic control: when a thermodynamically favored process has a very high activation
  energy, it proceeds too slowly to observe.
- Example: diamond converting to graphite is thermodynamically favored but kinetically
  extremely slow.
- A slow reaction is NOT necessarily at equilibrium; it may simply be kinetically controlled.


TOPIC 9.5 - FREE ENERGY AND EQUILIBRIUM

- deltaG degrees and K are directly related:
  EQN: deltaG degrees = -RT ln K
  EQN: K = e^(-deltaG degrees / RT)
  R = 8.314 J/(mol*K); T in Kelvin

- deltaG degrees < 0 -> K > 1 -> products favored at equilibrium.
- deltaG degrees > 0 -> K < 1 -> reactants favored at equilibrium.
- deltaG degrees = 0 -> K = 1 -> roughly equal amounts at equilibrium.
- When deltaG degrees is near 0: K is close to 1.
- When |deltaG degrees| >> RT: K is very large or very small.


TOPIC 9.6 - FREE ENERGY OF DISSOLUTION

- Dissolution of a salt involves three energetic factors:
  1. Breaking of solute-solute interactions (endothermic, decreases entropy).
  2. Reorganization of solvent around dissolved species (often endothermic for water).
  3. Formation of new solute-solvent interactions (exothermic, decreases entropy of solvent).
- The net deltaG for dissolution reflects the balance of all three.
- Predictions for total deltaG of dissolution are difficult because enthalpic and entropic
  contributions can partially cancel each other.


TOPIC 9.7 - COUPLED REACTIONS

- An external energy source can drive a thermodynamically unfavorable process:
  - Electrical energy drives electrolysis.
  - Light drives photosynthesis (CO2 + H2O -> glucose + O2).
- Coupled reactions: a thermodynamically unfavorable reaction is paired with a favorable
  one that shares an intermediate.
- The overall reaction has deltaG degrees < 0 (sum of both reactions is thermodynamically favored).
- Example in biology: ATP hydrolysis (deltaG << 0) is coupled to biosynthesis reactions
  (deltaG > 0) to drive them forward.


TOPIC 9.8 - GALVANIC (VOLTAIC) AND ELECTROLYTIC CELLS

Key vocabulary:
- Anode: electrode where OXIDATION occurs (OIL: Oxidation Is Loss of electrons).
- Cathode: electrode where REDUCTION occurs (RIG: Reduction Is Gain of electrons).
- "AN OX, RED CAT" mnemonic: ANOde = OXidation; REDuction = CAThode.

NOTE: Labeling electrodes as positive or negative will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.

Galvanic (voltaic) cell:
- Converts chemical energy to electrical energy.
- Reaction is thermodynamically favored (deltaG < 0, positive cell potential E degrees > 0).
- Electrons flow spontaneously from anode to cathode through external circuit.
- Ions flow through salt bridge to maintain electrical neutrality.
- Anode mass decreases (metal oxidized); cathode mass increases (metal deposited).

Electrolytic cell:
- Converts electrical energy to chemical energy.
- Reaction is thermodynamically unfavored (deltaG > 0, requires external voltage).
- Used for electroplating, charging batteries, electrolysis of water.

Salt bridge function: allows ions to flow between half-cells to balance charge;
cations flow toward cathode side, anions flow toward anode side.


TOPIC 9.9 - CELL POTENTIAL AND FREE ENERGY

Standard cell potential:
- EQN: E degrees(cell) = E degrees(cathode) - E degrees(anode)
  (using standard reduction potentials from table)
- More positive E degrees(cell): more thermodynamically favored reaction.

Relationship to Gibbs free energy:
- EQN: deltaG degrees = -n*F*E degrees(cell)
  n = number of moles of electrons transferred
  F = Faraday's constant = 96,485 C/mol electrons (approximately 96,500 C/mol)
  E degrees(cell) in volts (V = J/C)

- E degrees > 0 -> deltaG degrees < 0 -> thermodynamically favored.
- E degrees < 0 -> deltaG degrees > 0 -> thermodynamically unfavored.

Calculating standard reduction potentials:
- Reduction half-reaction with higher (more positive) E degrees = cathode.
- Reduction half-reaction with lower (more negative) E degrees = anode (reversed to oxidation).
- Standard reduction potential of the reversed (anode) half-reaction = -E degrees(anode, as written).
- Standard reduction potentials are NOT multiplied when half-reactions are scaled.


TOPIC 9.10 - CELL POTENTIAL UNDER NONSTANDARD CONDITIONS

Nernst equation:
- EQN: E = E degrees - (RT/nF) ln Q
- Cell potential E depends on concentrations of active species (via Q).

Key conceptual relationships:
- Standard conditions: Q = 1, so E = E degrees.
- As reaction proceeds toward equilibrium: |E| decreases.
- At equilibrium: Q = K and E = 0 (cell is "dead").
- Q < 1 (fewer products than at standard): |E| > |E degrees| (higher driving force).
- Q > 1 (more products than at standard): |E| < |E degrees| (lower driving force).

Concentration cell: both half-cells use the same redox couple but at different
concentrations. Electrons flow from the dilute side (anode) to the concentrated side
(cathode) to equalize concentrations.

NOTE: Quantitative calculations with the Nernst equation are NOT assessed on the
AP Exam; only qualitative understanding is required.


TOPIC 9.11 - ELECTROLYSIS AND FARADAY'S LAW

Faraday's Law connects charge flow to mass deposited/dissolved in electrolysis.

EQN: I = q/t
  I = current in amperes (A)
  q = charge in coulombs (C)
  t = time in seconds (s)

1 mole of electrons = 96,485 C (Faraday's constant, F)

Problem-solving steps:
1. Find charge: q = I x t (current x time)
2. Find moles of electrons: mol e- = q / F
3. Use stoichiometry to find moles of substance (based on electrons per ion/atom).
4. Convert to mass: mass = moles x molar mass.

Applications of Faraday's Law:
- Mass of metal deposited during electroplating.
- Mass of electrode that dissolves.
- Current required for a given rate of deposition.
- Time required for a given amount of deposition.

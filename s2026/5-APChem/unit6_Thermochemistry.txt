UNIT 6: THERMOCHEMISTRY
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
=========================

TOPIC 6.1 - ENDOTHERMIC AND EXOTHERMIC PROCESSES

- Temperature change indicates energy transfer between system and surroundings.
- Exothermic: system releases energy to surroundings; products are at lower energy
  than reactants; temperature of surroundings increases; deltaH < 0.
- Endothermic: system absorbs energy from surroundings; products are at higher energy
  than reactants; temperature of surroundings decreases; deltaH > 0.
- Dissolving: may be endothermic or exothermic depending on whether energy released
  in forming new solute-solvent interactions exceeds energy required to break
  solute-solute and solvent-solvent interactions.


TOPIC 6.2 - ENERGY DIAGRAMS

- Energy diagrams show the energy of reactants vs. products.
- Exothermic: products lower than reactants on energy diagram.
- Endothermic: products higher than reactants on energy diagram.
- Energy diagrams also show activation energy (hump between reactants and products).


TOPIC 6.3 - HEAT TRANSFER AND THERMAL EQUILIBRIUM

- Particles in a warmer body have higher average KE than particles in a cooler body.
- Heat transfer: collisions between particles in contact allow KE to transfer from hotter
  to cooler body.
- Thermal equilibrium: reached when both bodies reach the same temperature
  (same average KE).
- First Law of Thermodynamics: energy is conserved; energy lost by system = energy
  gained by surroundings (and vice versa).


TOPIC 6.4 - HEAT CAPACITY AND CALORIMETRY

EQN: q = mc(delta T)
  q = heat transferred (J or kJ)
  m = mass (g)
  c = specific heat capacity (J/g*K or J/g*degreeC)
  delta T = T(final) - T(initial) (in K or degreeC, same magnitude)

- Specific heat capacity (c): energy needed to raise 1 g of a substance by 1 K.
- Molar heat capacity: energy needed to raise 1 mol of a substance by 1 K.
- Water has a relatively high specific heat (4.18 J/g*K).
- Different substances have different specific heat capacities; equal masses of
  different substances change temperature differently for the same heat input.

Calorimetry:
- Calorimeter measures heat exchange in reactions or physical processes.
- Coffee-cup calorimeter (constant pressure): used for reactions in solution.
  q(reaction) = -q(solution) = -(m*c*delta T)
- Temperature increase: exothermic process (system releases heat to solution).
- Temperature decrease: endothermic process (system absorbs heat from solution).


TOPIC 6.5 - ENERGY OF PHASE CHANGES

- Phase transitions occur at constant temperature for a pure substance.
- Melting/vaporization: endothermic; system absorbs energy to break IMFs.
- Freezing/condensation: exothermic; system releases energy as IMFs form.

EQN: q = n x deltaH(phase change)
  n = moles of substance
  deltaH(fusion) = energy to melt one mole
  deltaH(vaporization) = energy to vaporize one mole

- deltaH(condensation) = -deltaH(vaporization) [equal magnitude, opposite sign]
- deltaH(freezing) = -deltaH(fusion)

Heating/cooling curve for a pure substance:
- Sloped portions: temperature is changing (using q = mc*deltaT).
- Flat portions: phase change at constant temperature (using q = n*deltaH).


TOPIC 6.6 - INTRODUCTION TO ENTHALPY OF REACTION

- Enthalpy change (deltaH rxn): heat absorbed or released at constant pressure.
  Negative deltaH: exothermic (heat released).
  Positive deltaH: endothermic (heat absorbed).

EQN: q = n x deltaH(rxn)
  n = moles of reaction as written

- Bonds breaking: endothermic (energy absorbed).
- Bonds forming: exothermic (energy released).
- Net deltaH = energy to break bonds in reactants - energy released forming bonds
  in products.

NOTE: Distinction between enthalpy (H) and internal energy (U) will NOT be tested.
At constant pressure (most AP reactions), deltaH = q.


TOPIC 6.7 - BOND ENTHALPIES

- Average bond enthalpy: average energy needed to break one mole of a specific bond
  in the gas phase.
- Estimated enthalpy of reaction using bond enthalpies:

EQN: deltaH(rxn) = sum of bond energies broken (reactants) - sum of bond energies
                   formed (products)

Mnemonic: "Bonds Broken = energy In; Bonds Formed = energy Out"
  deltaH = (sum of bonds broken) - (sum of bonds formed)
  If energy released in forming bonds > energy absorbed in breaking bonds: exothermic.

- Bond enthalpies give estimates; actual values can differ because they use averages.
- Stronger bonds have higher bond energies and shorter bond lengths.
- Triple bonds > double bonds > single bonds in energy.


TOPIC 6.8 - ENTHALPY OF FORMATION

- Standard enthalpy of formation (deltaHf degrees): enthalpy change when 1 mole of
  a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states.
- Standard state: pure substance at 1 atm; solutions at 1 M; gases at 1 atm.
- deltaHf degrees of a pure element in its standard state = 0.

EQN: deltaH(rxn) degrees = sum[deltaHf degrees (products)] - sum[deltaHf degrees (reactants)]

Steps:
1. Look up deltaHf degrees for each product and reactant.
2. Multiply each by stoichiometric coefficient.
3. Subtract sum of reactant values from sum of product values.


TOPIC 6.9 - HESS'S LAW

Hess's Law: Because enthalpy is a state function, the total enthalpy change for a
reaction is the same regardless of the pathway taken. Therefore:
deltaH(overall) = sum of deltaH for each step.

Rules for manipulating equations:
1. If a reaction is REVERSED, flip the sign of deltaH.
2. If a reaction is MULTIPLIED by a factor c, multiply deltaH by c.
3. If reactions are ADDED together, add their deltaH values.

Strategy:
- Identify the target reaction.
- Arrange given reactions so desired species appear as needed (as reactants or products).
- Flip and/or scale each equation accordingly, adjusting deltaH.
- Add all equations and deltaH values.


KEY EQUATIONS FOR UNIT 6
- q = mc(delta T)
- q = n x deltaH(phase change)
- q = n x deltaH(rxn)
- deltaH(rxn) degrees = sum[deltaHf degrees(products)] - sum[deltaHf degrees(reactants)]
- deltaH(rxn) = sum(bond energies broken) - sum(bond energies formed)

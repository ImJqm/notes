UNIT 5: KINETICS
AP Exam Weighting: 7-9%
=========================

TOPIC 5.1 - REACTION RATES

- Reaction rate: the change in concentration of a reactant or product per unit of time.
- Rate = -delta[reactant]/delta t = +delta[product]/delta t
- Rates of change of individual species are related by stoichiometry:
  For aA + bB -> cC + dD:
  Rate = -(1/a) x delta[A]/delta t = -(1/b) x delta[B]/delta t
       = +(1/c) x delta[C]/delta t = +(1/d) x delta[D]/delta t

- Factors that increase reaction rate:
  - Higher reactant concentration
  - Higher temperature
  - Greater surface area (for solids)
  - Addition of a catalyst


TOPIC 5.2 - INTRODUCTION TO RATE LAW

- Rate law: rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
  k = rate constant (temperature dependent)
  m = order with respect to A
  n = order with respect to B
  m + n = overall order of reaction

- Reaction orders (m, n) must be determined experimentally; they cannot be predicted
  from the balanced equation (unless the reaction is elementary).
- Method of initial rates: compare initial rates from experiments where only one
  reactant concentration changes at a time.
  If [A] doubles and rate doubles: first order in A (m = 1).
  If [A] doubles and rate quadruples: second order in A (m = 2).
  If [A] doubles and rate is unchanged: zeroth order in A (m = 0).

- Units of k depend on overall reaction order:
  Zeroth order: M/s
  First order: 1/s (or s^-1)
  Second order: 1/(M*s)


TOPIC 5.3 - CONCENTRATION CHANGES OVER TIME

Integrated rate laws (for a single reactant A):

Zeroth order: [A]t = [A]0 - kt
  EQN: [A]t - [A]0 = -kt
  Plot of [A] vs. t is linear; slope = -k

First order: ln[A]t = ln[A]0 - kt
  EQN: ln[A]t - ln[A]0 = -kt
  Plot of ln[A] vs. t is linear; slope = -k

Second order:
  EQN: 1/[A]t - 1/[A]0 = kt
  Plot of 1/[A] vs. t is linear; slope = k

To determine order from data: try plotting [A], ln[A], and 1/[A] vs. time;
the one that is linear tells you the order.

Half-life (first order only):
  EQN: t(1/2) = 0.693 / k
  For first order reactions, half-life is CONSTANT and independent of concentration.
  This applies to radioactive decay.

Half-life for zeroth order: t(1/2) = [A]0 / 2k (depends on initial concentration)
Half-life for second order: t(1/2) = 1 / (k[A]0) (depends on initial concentration)


TOPIC 5.4 - ELEMENTARY REACTIONS

- Elementary reaction: a single step describing an actual molecular collision.
- Molecularity: number of particles colliding in one elementary step.
  Unimolecular: A -> products, rate = k[A]
  Bimolecular: A + B -> products, rate = k[A][B]  (most common)
  Termolecular: A + B + C -> products (very rare)
- For an elementary reaction, the rate law CAN be written directly from stoichiometry.


TOPIC 5.5 - COLLISION MODEL

- Successful reaction requires a collision with:
  1. Sufficient energy (at or above activation energy, Ea).
  2. Proper orientation of the reacting molecules.

- Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution: shows the range of kinetic energies of particles at
  a given temperature. The fraction of particles with energy >= Ea determines reaction rate.
  At higher temperature: more particles have E >= Ea, so more successful collisions occur.


TOPIC 5.6 - REACTION ENERGY PROFILE

- Reaction coordinate diagram plots energy vs. progress of reaction.
- Reactants -> transition state (highest energy point) -> products
- Activation energy (Ea): energy difference between reactants and transition state.
  The higher Ea, the slower the reaction at a given temperature.
- deltaH (enthalpy change) = energy of products - energy of reactants
  Exothermic: products lower in energy than reactants; deltaH < 0.
  Endothermic: products higher in energy than reactants; deltaH > 0.

- Arrhenius equation: rate constant k is temperature-dependent; higher T means larger k.
  NOTE: Calculations involving the Arrhenius equation will NOT be assessed on the AP Exam.


TOPIC 5.7 - INTRODUCTION TO REACTION MECHANISMS

- Reaction mechanism: a series of elementary steps that together give the overall reaction.
- When elementary steps are added together, they must give the overall balanced equation.
- Reaction intermediate: produced in one step, consumed in a later step.
  Not present in the overall equation. Can be detected experimentally.
- Catalyst: present in the mechanism but not consumed overall (appears in overall equation
  as neither reactant nor product, though it may appear in elementary steps).


TOPIC 5.8 - REACTION MECHANISM AND RATE LAW

- Rate-determining step (rate-limiting step): the slowest step in the mechanism.
- When the first step is rate-determining (or all steps are irreversible):
  The overall rate law = rate law of the slowest elementary step.
- The overall rate law derived from mechanism must be consistent with the
  experimentally determined rate law.


TOPIC 5.9 - PRE-EQUILIBRIUM APPROXIMATION

- When the first step is a fast equilibrium (not the slow step), use the pre-equilibrium
  approximation to express the concentration of the intermediate in terms of reactants.
- Substitute this into the rate law of the slow step to get the overall rate law.
- The derived rate law must still match experimental observations.


TOPIC 5.10 - MULTISTEP REACTION ENERGY PROFILE

- Energy profile for a multistep reaction shows multiple humps, one for each step.
- Each hump corresponds to one transition state.
- Valleys between humps correspond to intermediates.
- The rate-determining step is the one with the HIGHEST activation energy (highest hump).
- Overall deltaH = energy of final products - energy of initial reactants.


TOPIC 5.11 - CATALYSIS

- A catalyst increases reaction rate WITHOUT being consumed overall.
- Catalysts work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a LOWER activation energy.
- Lower Ea means more particles have sufficient energy to react -> faster rate.
- Catalyst does NOT change deltaH (overall energy change) of the reaction.
- Homogeneous catalyst: same phase as reactants.
- Heterogeneous catalyst: different phase from reactants (e.g., solid catalyst, gas reactants).
- Catalyst appears as a reactant in one step and product in another step of the mechanism.


KEY EQUATIONS FOR UNIT 5
- Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
- Zeroth order: [A]t - [A]0 = -kt
- First order: ln[A]t - ln[A]0 = -kt
- Second order: 1/[A]t - 1/[A]0 = kt
- First order half-life: t(1/2) = 0.693/k

AP PHYSICS 1 - UNIT 3: WORK, ENERGY, AND POWER
Exam Weighting: 18-23%

=============================================================
TRANSLATIONAL KINETIC ENERGY
=============================================================
Kinetic energy (K) of a moving object:
  K = (1/2) * m * v^2

Kinetic energy is a scalar and is always >= 0.
Different observers in different reference frames may measure different KE values.

=============================================================
WORK
=============================================================
Work: energy transferred into or out of a system by a force acting over a displacement.
Work is a scalar; can be positive, negative, or zero.

Only the component of force PARALLEL to displacement does work:
  W = F_parallel * d = F * d * cos(theta)
  (theta = angle between force vector and displacement vector)

The component PERPENDICULAR to displacement changes direction of motion but NOT kinetic energy.

Work-Energy Theorem:
  Net work done on an object = change in kinetic energy of the object
  W_net = delta_K = K_final - K_initial

If only kinetic energy can change (system modeled as a single object):
  W_net = delta_K

Work done by friction is dissipated as thermal energy:
  Energy dissipated by friction = F_friction * path length

Work is equal to the area under the F_parallel vs. displacement graph.

Conservative forces: work is path-independent; only depends on initial and final positions.
  - Gravity and spring force are conservative.
  - If object returns to starting point, net work by conservative force = 0.
  - Potential energy is associated with conservative forces only.

Nonconservative forces: work is path-dependent (friction, air resistance).
  - Energy is dissipated (typically as thermal energy or sound).

=============================================================
POTENTIAL ENERGY
=============================================================
Potential energy: scalar energy associated with the configuration (position) of objects
in a system interacting via conservative forces.

A single object by itself has NO potential energy; you need a system of at least two objects.

Zero reference point for PE is chosen by the observer (choice should simplify the analysis).

Types of potential energy:

Elastic (spring) potential energy:
  U_s = (1/2) * k * x^2
  (k = spring constant; x = displacement from equilibrium/natural length)

Gravitational potential energy (general, two masses):
  U_g = -G * m1 * m2 / r
  (r = distance between centers of mass; G = 6.67 x 10^-11 N m^2/kg^2)

Gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface (constant g approximation):
  delta_U_g = m * g * delta_h   or   U_g = m * g * h   (with h measured from reference)
  (valid when change in height is small compared to Earth's radius)

For a system of more than two objects, total PE = sum of PE for each interacting pair.

=============================================================
CONSERVATION OF ENERGY
=============================================================
Mechanical energy = sum of kinetic and potential energies:
  E_mech = K + U

Energy is always conserved in any interaction.

If a system is defined so that:
  (1) No external work is done on the system, AND
  (2) No nonconservative interactions occur within the system
  THEN: total mechanical energy is constant (E_mech,initial = E_mech,final)

  K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f

If nonconservative forces (like friction) are present:
  K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f + |W_friction|
  (mechanical energy decreases by the work done by friction)

If work is done on the system by an external force:
  W_external = delta_E_mech

Energy can be transferred between kinetic and potential forms, or between system and environment.
Mechanical energy dissipated by nonconservative forces becomes thermal energy and/or sound.

Energy bar charts: useful tool to represent energy before and after an interaction.

=============================================================
POWER
=============================================================
Power: rate of energy transfer or conversion with respect to time.
  P_avg = W / delta_t = delta_E / delta_t

Units: Watts (W) = Joules/second (J/s)

Average power from work:
  P_avg = W_total / delta_t

Instantaneous power from a constant force:
  P = F_parallel * v   (force component parallel to velocity, times speed)

=============================================================
KEY EQUATIONS SUMMARY
=============================================================
K = (1/2) * m * v^2
W = F * d * cos(theta)
W_net = delta_K
U_spring = (1/2) * k * x^2
U_gravity (near surface) = m * g * h
U_gravity (general) = -G * m1 * m2 / r
E_mech = K + U
P = W / t = F * v (instantaneous, constant force)

=============================================================
KEY REMINDERS
=============================================================
- Work is done ONLY when the force has a component parallel to displacement; perpendicular
  force components do no work (e.g., normal force on horizontal surface does zero work).
- A system consisting of ONLY a single object can have kinetic energy only, NOT potential energy.
- Potential energy belongs to the SYSTEM, not to any single object.
- The choice of zero height for gravitational PE is arbitrary; only CHANGES in PE matter.
- Friction ALWAYS reduces mechanical energy; energy is not destroyed but becomes thermal energy.
- Conservation of energy applies to all interactions; if mechanical energy decreases, look for
  where it went (heat, sound, deformation).
- The work-energy theorem applies to ALL forces, while conservation of mechanical energy applies
  when nonconservative forces do zero work.

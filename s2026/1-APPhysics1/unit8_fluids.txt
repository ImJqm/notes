AP PHYSICS 1 - UNIT 8: FLUIDS
Exam Weighting: 10-15%

=============================================================
INTERNAL STRUCTURE AND DENSITY
=============================================================
Matter states:
  Solids: atoms/molecules tightly bound, fixed shape.
  Liquids: atoms/molecules loosely bound, no fixed shape.
  Gases: atoms/molecules far apart, no fixed shape.
  Fluids = liquids + gases (no fixed shape).

Ideal fluid: incompressible (constant density) and has no viscosity (no internal friction).

Density:
  rho = m / V   (mass per unit volume; units: kg/m^3)

  - Density is a property of a material.
  - More dense substance: more mass per same volume.
  - Volume and density of an incompressible fluid do not change with pressure.

=============================================================
PRESSURE
=============================================================
Pressure: magnitude of perpendicular force per unit area.
  P = F_perp / A

  - Pressure is a SCALAR (no direction).
  - Units: Pascals (Pa) = N/m^2

Pressure in a fluid is caused by the collective forces of the fluid's particles on surfaces.

Absolute pressure at a point in a fluid:
  P_abs = P_0 + P_gauge
  where P_0 = reference pressure (e.g., atmospheric pressure P_atm)
  and P_gauge = gauge pressure (additional pressure due to fluid column)

Gauge pressure from a vertical column of fluid:
  P_gauge = rho * g * h
  where rho = fluid density, g = 10 m/s^2, h = depth below fluid surface

Therefore: P_abs = P_0 + rho * g * h

Key consequences:
  - Pressure increases with depth in a fluid.
  - Pressure at the same depth is the same throughout a connected static fluid (Pascal's Principle implied).
  - Pressure does NOT depend on horizontal position or the shape of the container.

=============================================================
FLUIDS AND NEWTON'S LAWS
=============================================================
Newton's laws apply to fluid particles.
The macroscopic behavior of a fluid results from internal particle interactions and external forces.

A fluid accelerates (velocity changes) when there is a net force on it, including pressure differences.

BUOYANT FORCE
An object submerged in or floating in a fluid experiences an upward buoyant force:
  - Result of fluid pushing harder on the bottom of the object than on the top (pressure increases with depth).
  - Arises from the collective forces of fluid particles on the object.

Archimedes' Principle:
  F_B = rho_fluid * g * V_displaced
  (buoyant force = weight of fluid displaced by the object)

  - Buoyant force is UPWARD.
  - Only depends on the density of the fluid and volume of fluid displaced (NOT on object material or density).
  - V_displaced = volume of the part of the object that is submerged.

Floating condition:
  - Object floats when buoyant force = weight of object.
  - F_B = m_object * g  =>  rho_fluid * g * V_displaced = rho_object * g * V_object
  - Fraction submerged = rho_object / rho_fluid
  - If rho_object < rho_fluid: object floats (partially submerged).
  - If rho_object > rho_fluid: object sinks.
  - If rho_object = rho_fluid: object is neutrally buoyant (floats fully submerged).

For a submerged object:
  Net upward force = F_B - F_gravity = (rho_fluid - rho_object) * g * V
  If rho_fluid > rho_object: net force upward (object rises).
  If rho_fluid < rho_object: net force downward (object sinks).

=============================================================
FLUIDS AND CONSERVATION LAWS
=============================================================
CONTINUITY EQUATION (Conservation of Mass for Fluids)
For an incompressible fluid flowing through a tube:
  - The rate at which mass enters must equal the rate at which mass exits.
  - Flow rate (volume per second): Q = A * v
    where A = cross-sectional area, v = fluid speed

Continuity equation:
  A_1 * v_1 = A_2 * v_2

  - Fluid speeds up where the tube narrows (smaller area => faster flow).
  - Fluid slows down where the tube widens.

BERNOULLI'S EQUATION (Conservation of Energy for Fluids)
For an ideal fluid in steady flow:
  P + (1/2) * rho * v^2 + rho * g * h = constant   (same at all points along a streamline)

  Where: P = pressure at a point, v = fluid speed, h = height of the point

Bernoulli's equation says that along a streamline, total mechanical energy per unit volume is constant.
  - Faster flow => lower pressure (e.g., airplane wing lift, venturi effect).
  - Higher elevation => lower pressure (if speed is constant).
  - Lower elevation => higher pressure (consistent with P = P_0 + rho*g*h for static case).

Torricelli's Theorem (derived from Bernoulli; open container with hole):
  Speed of fluid exiting a small hole at depth h below the surface:
  v = sqrt(2 * g * h)
  (derived by setting v_top = 0, P_top = P_hole = atmospheric, and applying Bernoulli)

BOUNDARY: All fluids are ideal (incompressible, no viscosity) and pipes are full unless stated otherwise.

=============================================================
KEY EQUATIONS SUMMARY
=============================================================
rho = m / V
P = F / A
P_abs = P_0 + rho * g * h
F_B = rho_fluid * g * V_displaced   (Archimedes' Principle)
A_1 * v_1 = A_2 * v_2   (Continuity equation)
P + (1/2) * rho * v^2 + rho * g * h = constant   (Bernoulli's equation)
v_exit = sqrt(2 * g * h)   (Torricelli's theorem)

=============================================================
KEY REMINDERS
=============================================================
- Objects float NOT because they are "lighter" but because their density is less than the fluid's density.
- Buoyant force depends on VOLUME DISPLACED, not on the object's density or mass directly.
- A fully submerged object always displaces a volume of fluid equal to its own volume.
- Pressure acts equally in all directions at a given point in a static fluid.
- In Bernoulli's equation: faster flow = lower pressure (inverse relationship between v and P).
- Continuity: where a pipe is narrower, fluid moves faster (same volume per second must pass through).
- Torricelli's result is the same speed as an object in free fall from height h (no coincidence; same energy).
- Gauge pressure = 0 at the surface (where h = 0); absolute pressure at the surface = atmospheric pressure.
- Bernoulli's equation is essentially conservation of energy per unit volume for steady, ideal fluid flow.

AP PHYSICS 1 - UNIT 5: TORQUE AND ROTATIONAL DYNAMICS
Exam Weighting: 10-15%

=============================================================
ROTATIONAL KINEMATICS
=============================================================
Rigid system: holds its shape; different points move in different directions during rotation.
A rigid system CANNOT be modeled as a single object (because different parts move differently).

Angular displacement (theta): angle rotated (in radians).
  One direction (CW or CCW) is positive; the other is negative.

Average angular velocity (omega):
  omega_avg = delta_theta / delta_t   (radians per second)

Average angular acceleration (alpha):
  alpha_avg = delta_omega / delta_t   (radians per second squared)

For CONSTANT angular acceleration (exact analogues of linear kinematic equations):
  omega = omega_0 + alpha * t
  theta = theta_0 + omega_0 * t + (1/2) * alpha * t^2
  omega^2 = omega_0^2 + 2 * alpha * (theta - theta_0)

Graphs of angular displacement, velocity, and acceleration vs. time follow the same
relationships as linear kinematics graphs (slopes and areas).

BOUNDARY: Direction of rotation is described only as clockwise or counterclockwise
with respect to a given axis of rotation.

=============================================================
CONNECTING LINEAR AND ROTATIONAL MOTION
=============================================================
For a point at distance r from the axis of rotation:

  Arc length: s = r * theta   (theta in radians)
  Tangential speed: v = r * omega
  Tangential acceleration: a_t = r * alpha
  Centripetal acceleration: a_c = v^2 / r = r * omega^2   (toward center)

All points on a rigid system share the same angular velocity and angular acceleration.
Points farther from the axis have greater tangential speed and acceleration.

BOUNDARY: Rotation directions limited to clockwise/counterclockwise about a given axis.

=============================================================
TORQUE
=============================================================
Torque: the rotational effect of a force; causes angular acceleration.

Only the component of force PERPENDICULAR to the position vector (from axis to point of
application) produces torque:
  tau = r * F * sin(theta)
  where theta = angle between force vector and position vector from axis to application point
  OR equivalently: tau = F * L   where L = lever arm

Lever arm (L): the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the LINE OF ACTION
of the force. To maximize torque, apply force perpendicular to the moment arm (sin(90)=1).

Force diagrams (like FBDs): show forces AND their locations relative to the axis of rotation.

BOUNDARY: AP Physics 1 requires calculating the magnitude of torque but NOT the direction
(clockwise vs. counterclockwise convention is sufficient for direction description).

=============================================================
ROTATIONAL INERTIA
=============================================================
Rotational inertia (I): measures a system's resistance to changes in rotation.
Depends on both mass and how that mass is distributed relative to the rotation axis.

For a point mass at perpendicular distance r from axis:
  I = m * r^2

For a collection of objects:
  I_total = sum of (m_i * r_i^2)

Rotational inertia is MINIMUM when the axis passes through the center of mass.

Parallel Axis Theorem (when axis does NOT pass through center of mass):
  I = I_cm + M * d^2
  (I_cm = rotational inertia about axis through center of mass; d = distance between axes)

Qualitative understanding: mass farther from axis = greater rotational inertia.
  Example: hoop has more rotational inertia than solid disk of same mass and radius.

BOUNDARY: Students must know I for collections of up to five or fewer point masses in a 2D
configuration. Rotational inertia formulas for extended objects are GIVEN on the exam.

=============================================================
ROTATIONAL EQUILIBRIUM AND NEWTON'S FIRST LAW (ROTATIONAL)
=============================================================
Net torque = vector sum of all torques about a chosen axis.

Rotational equilibrium: net torque = 0.
  Sum of all torques = 0

Newton's First Law (rotational form): if net torque = 0, angular velocity is constant
(object continues rotating at the same rate, or remains at rest rotationally).

A system can be in rotational equilibrium without being in translational equilibrium, and
vice versa. Both conditions must be separately satisfied for complete static equilibrium.

=============================================================
NEWTON'S SECOND LAW IN ROTATIONAL FORM
=============================================================
If net torque is NOT zero, angular velocity changes:
  tau_net = I * alpha

  - alpha is in the same direction as tau_net.
  - alpha is proportional to tau_net.
  - alpha is inversely proportional to I.

This is the rotational analogue of F_net = m * a.

For a rotating rigid system, both linear and rotational analyses may be needed independently.

=============================================================
KEY EQUATIONS SUMMARY
=============================================================
omega_avg = delta_theta / delta_t
alpha_avg = delta_omega / delta_t
Rotational kinematic equations (constant alpha, analogous to linear)
s = r * theta;   v = r * omega;   a_t = r * alpha
tau = r * F * sin(theta) = F * L (lever arm)
I = m * r^2 (point mass)
I_total = sum of m_i * r_i^2
I = I_cm + M * d^2 (parallel axis theorem)
tau_net = I * alpha (Newton's 2nd Law, rotational)

=============================================================
KEY REMINDERS
=============================================================
- Torque depends on BOTH force magnitude AND where/how it is applied (lever arm).
- A force directed through the axis of rotation produces ZERO torque.
- Rotating at constant angular velocity means net torque = 0 (just like constant velocity means net force = 0).
- For a rigid system, every point has the same omega and alpha, but different tangential speeds (v = r*omega).
- When solving problems with both rotation and translation (e.g., a yo-yo), analyze each separately.
- For static equilibrium: BOTH net force = 0 AND net torque = 0.
- The choice of axis for torque calculations is arbitrary; choose a point that eliminates unknowns.

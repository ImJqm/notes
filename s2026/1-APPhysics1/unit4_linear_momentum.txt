AP PHYSICS 1 - UNIT 4: LINEAR MOMENTUM
Exam Weighting: 10-15%

=============================================================
LINEAR MOMENTUM
=============================================================
Linear momentum (vector quantity, same direction as velocity):
  p = m * v

Total momentum of a system = sum of individual momenta:
  p_total = sum of (m_i * v_i)

Momentum is used to analyze collisions and explosions.

Collision: interaction where forces between objects are much larger than external forces.
  - Analyze only initial and final states; use object model.

Explosion: internal forces in a system push objects apart.

NOTE: "momentum" in AP Physics 1 always means linear momentum.

=============================================================
CHANGE IN MOMENTUM AND IMPULSE
=============================================================
Change in momentum:
  delta_p = p_final - p_initial = m * v_final - m * v_initial

Relationship between net force and momentum (Newton's Second Law in momentum form):
  F_net = delta_p / delta_t

Impulse (J): product of average net force and the time interval over which it acts.
  J = F_avg * delta_t

Impulse is a VECTOR; same direction as the net force.

Impulse-Momentum Theorem:
  J = delta_p
  F_avg * delta_t = m * delta_v

Graphical interpretation:
  - Impulse = area under the F_net vs. time graph.
  - Net force = slope of the p vs. time graph.

Newton's Second Law is a result of the impulse-momentum theorem for constant mass:
  F_net = m * a   (derived from F_net = delta_p / delta_t when mass is constant)

Larger delta_t for same impulse means smaller average force (crumple zones, catching a ball gently).

BOUNDARY: AP Physics 1 does NOT include situations where mass changes with time.

=============================================================
CONSERVATION OF LINEAR MOMENTUM
=============================================================
Total momentum of a system is always conserved (in ALL interactions).

If net external force on the system = 0:
  p_total,initial = p_total,final   (momentum is constant)
  sum of (m_i * v_i,initial) = sum of (m_i * v_i,final)

If net external force is nonzero:
  momentum is transferred between the system and its environment.
  delta_p_system = J_external = F_external * delta_t

By Newton's Third Law: the impulse that object A exerts on B equals and opposes the
impulse that B exerts on A. Internal forces therefore cancel; they cannot change total momentum.

Velocity of center of mass:
  v_cm = (sum of m_i * v_i) / (sum of m_i)

In absence of external forces, v_cm is constant.

BOUNDARY: AP Physics 1 requires full quantitative treatment of conservation of momentum
in ONE dimension. In two dimensions, only a semiquantitative (setup) treatment is required
(solve simultaneous equations NOT required; but students must set them up and reason about changes).

=============================================================
ELASTIC AND INELASTIC COLLISIONS
=============================================================
Elastic collision:
  - Total kinetic energy is conserved (K_initial = K_final for the system).
  - Individual objects may gain or lose KE; the total is the same.
  - Momentum is always conserved.

Inelastic collision:
  - Total kinetic energy of the system DECREASES.
  - Some KE is converted to thermal energy, sound, or deformation.
  - Momentum is still conserved.

Perfectly inelastic collision:
  - Objects stick together and move with the same final velocity.
  - Maximum KE is lost (while still conserving momentum).
  - m_1 * v_1,i + m_2 * v_2,i = (m_1 + m_2) * v_f

Summary: Momentum is conserved in ALL collisions. KE is conserved ONLY in elastic collisions.

Momentum bar charts: useful representation for showing momentum before and after interactions.

=============================================================
KEY EQUATIONS SUMMARY
=============================================================
p = m * v
F_net = delta_p / delta_t
J = F_avg * delta_t
J = delta_p   (Impulse-Momentum Theorem)
p_total,i = p_total,f   (when no net external force)

=============================================================
KEY REMINDERS
=============================================================
- Momentum is a VECTOR; direction matters. Use sign conventions for 1D problems.
- A rubber ball bouncing off a wall has a LARGER momentum change than a clay ball that sticks,
  even if both hit with the same speed (because rubber ball reverses direction).
- Energy is NOT always conserved in collisions; momentum always is.
- A large force for a short time can have the same impulse as a small force for a long time.
- After a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects move together (one combined object).
- In an isolated system (no external force), total momentum never changes, but KE can change.

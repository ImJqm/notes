AP PHYSICS 1 - UNIT 2: FORCE AND TRANSLATIONAL DYNAMICS
Exam Weighting: 18-23%

=============================================================
SYSTEMS AND CENTER OF MASS
=============================================================
A system is a collection of objects. System properties come from interactions between
constituent objects. If internal structure is unimportant, the system can be treated as
a single object at its center of mass.

Internal forces (between objects within the system) do NOT affect motion of center of mass.
External forces DO affect motion of center of mass.

Center of mass location along one axis:
  x_cm = (sum of m_i * x_i) / (sum of m_i)

For symmetrical mass distributions, center of mass lies on the line(s) of symmetry.
BOUNDARY: Center of mass calculations are limited to highly symmetrical objects.

=============================================================
FORCES AND FREE-BODY DIAGRAMS (FBDs)
=============================================================
Forces are vector quantities describing interactions between objects/systems.
Every force on an object is exerted BY another object (forces come in pairs).
Contact forces are macroscopic effects of interatomic electric forces.

Free-body diagram rules:
  - Represent the object/system as a dot (at center of mass).
  - Draw each force as a straight arrow originating from the dot.
  - Arrow direction = direction of the force.
  - Forces in the same direction are drawn side by side, NOT overlapping.
  - Do NOT include component arrows on an FBD; show actual forces only.

Choose a coordinate system with one axis parallel to acceleration to simplify math.
  (e.g., for inclined plane, one axis parallel to the slope surface)

=============================================================
NEWTON'S THIRD LAW
=============================================================
Every force exerted by object A on object B has an equal and opposite force exerted
by object B on object A. These paired forces are the same type, same magnitude,
opposite direction, and act on DIFFERENT objects.

Tension: the force transmitted through a string, rope, chain, or cable.
  - Ideal string: negligible mass, does not stretch; tension is same throughout.
  - Non-ideal string with mass: tension may vary along its length (greater at top when hanging).
  - Ideal pulley: negligible mass, no friction at axle.

BOUNDARY: Distance interactions in AP Physics 1 are limited to gravitational forces only
(no electric or magnetic forces at a distance).

=============================================================
NEWTON'S FIRST LAW
=============================================================
Net force = vector sum of all forces on the system.
Translational equilibrium: net force = zero.
  Sum of all forces = 0 (equilibrium condition)

Newton's First Law: if net force = 0, velocity is constant (object stays at rest or
moves in a straight line at constant speed).

Forces can be balanced in one dimension but unbalanced in another; velocity only
changes in the direction of the unbalanced force.

Inertial reference frame: a frame in which Newton's first law holds.

=============================================================
NEWTON'S SECOND LAW
=============================================================
Net force is not zero means the system accelerates.

Newton's Second Law: F_net = m * a

  - a is in the same direction as F_net.
  - a is proportional to F_net and inversely proportional to mass.
  - Only net EXTERNAL forces change velocity of the center of mass.

=============================================================
GRAVITATIONAL FORCE
=============================================================
Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
  F_g = G * m_1 * m_2 / r^2

  - G = 6.67 x 10^-11 N m^2/kg^2
  - r = distance between centers of mass
  - Force is attractive, directed along the line connecting centers of mass
  - Force decreases with square of distance (inverse square law)

Gravitational field strength g:
  g = G * M / r^2  (magnitude at distance r from mass M)
  Near Earth's surface: g = 10 N/kg (or 10 m/s^2)

Weight = gravitational force on an object near a large body:
  F_g = m * g   (weight; directed downward near Earth)

If gravity is the only force: observed acceleration = gravitational field strength numerically.

Apparent weight = magnitude of normal force exerted on an object.
  - If accelerating: apparent weight differs from true gravitational force.
  - Weightless: when gravity is the only force acting (free fall), OR no forces act.

Equivalence principle: an observer in a noninertial frame cannot distinguish between
apparent weight and gravitational force.

Inertial mass vs gravitational mass: both measure the same property; experimentally equivalent.

=============================================================
KINETIC AND STATIC FRICTION
=============================================================
Kinetic friction: occurs when two surfaces slide relative to each other.
  - Direction: opposes relative motion of surfaces.
  - Does NOT depend on contact surface area.
  F_f,k = mu_k * F_n
  (mu_k = coefficient of kinetic friction; F_n = normal force)

Normal force: perpendicular force from a surface on an object, directed away from surface.

Static friction: prevents relative motion between surfaces not sliding.
  - Adjusts in magnitude/direction as needed (up to a maximum).
  F_f,s <= mu_s * F_n
  Maximum static friction: F_f,s,max = mu_s * F_n

Typically: mu_s > mu_k for the same surfaces.

=============================================================
SPRING FORCES (HOOKE'S LAW)
=============================================================
Ideal spring: negligible mass; force proportional to displacement from relaxed length.

Hooke's Law:
  F_s = k * x
  (k = spring constant in N/m; x = displacement from equilibrium/natural length)

The spring force always points TOWARD the equilibrium position (restoring force).

=============================================================
CIRCULAR MOTION
=============================================================
Object moving in a circle has centripetal acceleration directed TOWARD the center.
  a_c = v^2 / r   (v = speed, r = radius)

Net centripetal force (net force toward center):
  F_c = m * v^2 / r

Tangential acceleration: rate of change of speed, directed tangent to circle.
Net acceleration = vector sum of centripetal + tangential acceleration.

Period T: time for one complete revolution.
Frequency f: revolutions per second.
  T = 1/f
  T = 2 * pi * r / v   (for constant speed circular motion)

Uniform circular motion: constant speed, only centripetal acceleration.

Vertical circular loop:
  - At top of loop, minimum speed: gravity alone provides centripetal force.
    F_g = m * v_min^2 / r  =>  v_min = sqrt(g * r)

Banked curves: components of normal force and static friction contribute to centripetal force.
Conical pendulum: component of tension provides centripetal force.

Kepler's Third Law (circular orbits):
  For satellite in circular orbit, gravity provides centripetal force.
  T^2 / r^3 = 4 * pi^2 / (G * M)
  (M = mass of central body; the period and radius are related to central body's mass)

BOUNDARY: Banked curve with friction is qualitative only; frictionless banked curves can
be analyzed quantitatively. Kepler's third law is limited to circular orbits.

=============================================================
KEY REMINDERS
=============================================================
- Internal forces between objects in a system do NOT affect the system's center of mass motion.
- Newton's Third Law pairs act on DIFFERENT objects; they never cancel each other.
- Normal force is NOT always equal to weight (it equals weight only on flat, non-accelerating surfaces).
- Friction always opposes relative motion (kinetic) or tendency to slide (static).
- For circular motion problems, always identify what force(s) provide(s) centripetal acceleration.
- For FBDs on inclined planes, tilt the coordinate system along the slope to simplify components.
